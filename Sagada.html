CULTURE OF SAGADA

TRADITION- SAGADA

The people of sagada follow a unique burial ritual. The elderly carve their own coffins out of hollowed logs. If they are too weak or ill, their families prepare their coffins instead. The dead are placed inside their coffins (sometimes breaking their bones in the process of fitting them in), and the coffins are brought to a cave for burial.

BELIEFS- SAGADA

One of the most common beliefs behind hanging coffins of Sagada is that moving the bodies of the dead higher up brings them closer to their ancestral spirits. Moreover, people were afraid to be burried in the ground as the dogs would destroy the body and the wet soil would make it quickly decompose.

ARTS(Santiago Bose's mural)
- SAGADA

Santiago Bose's mural in Sagada is a cultural landmark that has been reimagined several times over the past 40 years. The mural, which is located near the entrance of Sagada, features a mix of traditional and contemporary motifs that depict the rich cultural heritage of the Cordillera region.

ARTS(Tinagtago as Scrap-Metal) - PALAWAN

A tinagtago as scrap-metal version of the bulul is a One of the most notable art pieces in Sagada is the tinagtago, a scrap-metal version of the bulul, a traditional Ifugao carving that represents the rice god. The tinagtago is the brainchild of Dario Frias Jr., a local artist who specializes in creating sculptures out of recycled materials.

ARTS(Dragon Sculpture)
- SAGADA

The dragon sculpture by Dario Frias Jr. is another noteworthy art piece in Sagada. The sculpture is a towering, multi-colored dragon made entirely out of scrap metal. It is located in the town's main square and serves as a popular spot for tourists to take photos.
